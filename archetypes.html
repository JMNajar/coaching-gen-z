Athlete Archetype Guide

Five athlete archetypes coaches encounter most often. Identify what you're seeing, then use the response that's already built for it.

The Overthinking Athlete
Over-processes every technical detail before and during reps
Symptoms
  • Hesitation before every rep
  • Inconsistent timing despite knowing the technique
  • Lack of aggression or commitment at the critical moment
  • Visible frustration after technically incorrect feeling reps
Modern Response
Simplify everything. Give them fewer thoughts — not more.
“Same run. Same takeoff.”  ·  “Mid-mark.”  ·  “One focus.”
Avoid
More technical information — giving more corrections makes it worse
Overthinking athletes rarely need more information. They need fewer thoughts.
The Resistant Athlete
Pushes back against correction — argues, dismisses, or ignores
Symptoms
  • Argues with instruction or body language signals disagreement
  • Eye-rolls or visible dismissal of correction
  • Maintains their own approach regardless
  • Frustration escalates when corrected repeatedly
Modern Response
Don't argue. Let them feel the correction — not hear it.
“Try it once.”  ·  “Give me one rep this way.”  ·  “What are you noticing?”
Avoid
Power struggles / Repeating the same correction louder
Athletes believe what they feel — not what they are told. Get them to experience the correction.
The Athlete Who Shuts Down
Emotionally overwhelmed — withdraws from the practice environment
Symptoms
  • Silence — stops responding to instruction
  • Drops eye contact, turns away
  • Stops attempting reps or goes through motions
  • Emotional withdrawal visible in body language
Modern Response
Deploy the Reset Protocol. Simplify to one controllable action.
“First three steps only.”  ·  “One focus.”  ·  “Reset.”
Avoid
Louder correction / More information / Any visible frustration
Overwhelmed athletes need simplicity — not intensity. The more overwhelmed, the simpler coaching must become.
The Low Confidence Athlete
Afraid of failure — performs cautiously and avoids commitment
Symptoms
  • Cautious, hesitant movement at key moments
  • Inconsistent execution — holds back
  • Lack of aggression when commitment is required
  • Avoids new attempts or heights
Modern Response
Remove outcome pressure completely. Build through small wins.
“Today we only care about clean rhythm.”  ·  “Give me one clean rep.”  ·  “Repeat that execution.”
Avoid
“We need bigger jumps.” / Any outcome-focused language
Confidence grows through successful repetition — not speeches. Build small wins every single practice.
The Distracted Athlete
Cannot maintain focus through practice — attention shifts constantly
Symptoms
  • Checking phone or environment between reps
  • Late to respond to instruction
  • Short attention window on reps
  • Inconsistent effort across the session
Modern Response
Shorten communication. Don't fight their attention — adapt to it.
“Eyes up.”  ·  “Hit your mark.”  ·  “Same rhythm.”
Avoid
“Wake up.” / Long technical explanations / Criticizing their focus directly
Attention improves when instruction becomes immediately actionable. Don't lecture — cue.
